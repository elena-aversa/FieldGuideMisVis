Data visualisations
circulating on social media
can mislead.

They can give the impression that there is only one way to interpret the facts, especially concerning sensitive topics. However, data visualisations are not value-neutral or independent of biases. They represent one of many possible interpretations of reality.

Misleading data visualisations can be harmful, and thus being critical is essential. With this Field Guide, you can explore and recognise the threats posed by misleading data visualisations circulating on social media during social crises.

You will be doing so by exploring a collection of 20 data visualisations shared about the Russo-Ukrainian conflict.

✹ HOW TO SPOT MISLEADING DATA VISUALISATIONS ON SOCIAL MEDIA?

The process is not immediate and assumes 👀 seeing things differently 👀: looking at data visualisations with a critical mindset. It means questioning, analysing, interpreting, and evaluating each data visualisation.

A data visualisation shared on social netwoks typically travels through posts 💬. There are 3 important elements to consider about these posts, and are reflected in their structure.

So! Before drawing your own conclusions, carefully consider the following three elements when you approach a data visualisation on social media.

👉🏼 Tip: Expand the three sections in sequential order, and explore further details of each section following the same order.

1. THE MISLEADING POTENTIAL OF THE DATA VISUALISATION

Take a closer look at how information is visually presented: misleading tactics in design process can make a data visualisation misleading!

The process of designing a data visualisation consists of iterative steps, which can vary depending on the specific case.

However, they can be broadly grouped in three main categories:

🔧 working with the data,
🪄 visualising the data,
💅🏽 finalising the data visualisation.

In these three stages, it can happen that, with or without intention, the designer of the data visualisation applies some misleading tactics, which may render the data visualisation misleading.

The number of tactics applied define the misleading potential of a data visualisation.

2. THE POSITION OF THE SOCIAL MEDIA POST

Pay attention to the position of the social media post: a misleading data visualisation can be presented as reliable!

When shared online, data visualisations can be used with different positions. The same data visualisation can be used to support even opposing viewpoints.

The positions taken can vary in detail, but can be broadly grouped in:

✅ confirming post,
📋 descriptive post,
🤨 sceptical post.

Always be critical of the text accompanying a visualisation, and assess the misleading potential of the data visualisation before trusting its message.

3. THE POSITION OF THE USERS' COMMENTS

Observe what users comment under the social media post: often users expose the misleading nature of the data visualisation.

Similarly to the position of the post. Some users may support the message shared with the misleading data visualisation, while others may reveal its true nature.

The positions taken can vary in detail but can be broadly categorised into:

✅ confirming comment,
📋 descriptive comment,
🤨 sceptical comment,
❓ other comment.

Always be critical of the comments on a data visualisation, try to consider different points of view, and remain open to various possibilities.
