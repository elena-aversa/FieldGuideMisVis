Data visualisations
circulating on social media
can mislead.

They can give the impression that there is only one way to interpret the facts, especially concerning sensitive topics. However, data visualisations are not value-neutral or independent of biases. They represent one of many possible interpretations of reality.

Misleading data visualisations can be harmful, and thus being critical is essential. With this Field Guide, you can explore and recognise the threats posed by misleading data visualisations circulating on social media during social crises.

1. THE MISLEADING POTENTIAL OF THE DATA VISUALISATION

Take a closer look at how information is visually presented.

‚ö†Ô∏è Misleading tactics design process can make a data visualisation misleading.

The process of designing a data visualisation consists of iterative steps, which can vary depending on the specific case. However, they can be broadly grouped in three main categories:

WORKING WITH THE DATA

In this stage, the designer of the visualisation acquires, organises, filters, and analyses the data for the visualisations.

VISUALISING THE DATA

In this stage, the designer decides how to visualise the selected data.

FINALISING THE DATA VISUALISATION

In this stage, the designer improves the output of the previous stage by adding, removing or editing the visual elements.

In these three stages, it can happen that, with or without intention, the designer of the data visualisation applies some misleading tactics, which may render the data visualisation misleading. The number of tactics applied define the misleading potential of a data visualisation.

2. THE POSITION OF THE SOCIAL MEDIA POST

Pay attention to the position of the social media post.

‚ö†Ô∏è A misleading data visualisation can be presented as reliable.

When shared online, data visualisations can be used with different positions. The same data visualisation can be used to support even opposing viewpoints. The positions taken can vary in detail, but can be broadly grouped in:

CONFIRMING POST

Posts supporting or agreeing with the information in the data visualisation often show clear backing. They explain why the visualisation is seen as reliable or makes sense. In short, the text believes in what the visualisation shows and supports its story.

DESCRIPTIVE POST

Posts that don't clearly pick sides about the info in the data visualisation. Instead, they describe specific situations, details, or the visualisation itself. These posts give a neutral view or thoughts on different parts of the visualisation.

SCEPTICAL POST

Posts that challenge or question the info in the data visualisation. They might outright say the visualisation is misleading, not trustworthy, or has issues, or simply disagree with what the post is saying.

Always be critical of the text accompanying a visualisation, and assess the misleading potential of the data visualisation before trusting its message.

3. THE POSITION OF THE USERS' COMMENTS

Observe what users comment under the social media post.

‚ö†Ô∏è Often users expose the misleading nature of the data visualisation.

Similarly to the position of the post. Some users may support the message shared with the misleading data visualisation, while others may reveal its true nature. The positions taken can vary in detail but can be broadly categorised into:

CONFIRMING COMMENT

Comments supporting or agreeing with the information in the data visualisation often show clear backing. They explain why the visualisation is seen as reliable or makes sense. In short, the text believes in what the visualisation shows and supports its story.

DESCRIPTIVE COMMENT

Comments that don't clearly pick sides about the info in the data visualisation. Instead, they describe specific situations, details, or the visualisation itself or ask question with the only intent to know more. These comments give a neutral view or thoughts on different parts of the visualisation

SCEPTICAL COMMENT

Comments that challenge or question the info in the data visualisation or oppose to the narrative of the post. They might outright say the visualisation is misleading, not trustworthy, or has issues, or simply disagree with what the post is saying. They might use irony or other media, such as memes.

Always be critical of the comments on a data visualisation, try to consider different points of view, and remain open to various possibilities.
