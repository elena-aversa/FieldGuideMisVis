Misleading tactics
in the process of designing
a data visualisation

A misleading data visualisation can be the result of misleading tactics being applied during the visualisation process.

The number of tactics applied defines the   ü¶π misleading potential ü¶π   of a data visualisation.

The greater the number of these tactics employed, the higher the misleading potential of the data visualisation.

The misleading potential includes up to 20 misleading tactics so far.

To understand the total misleading potential score of a data visualisation, remember: each confirmed misleading tactic scores 1 point, and each tactic that might be used scores 0.5.

The total score defines the misleading potential of the data visualisation.

SCORE MISLEADING POTENTIAL
1 to 4.5 Low misleading
5 to 8.5 Moderately misleading
9 to 12.5 Misleading
13 to 16.5 Highly misleading
17 to 20 Extremely misleading

Below, you can explore all 20 misleading tactics covered in this field guide, complete with descriptions and examples.

The tactics are categorised into three stages of the data visualisation process, depending on when they occur in such process.

üîß 1. WORKING WITH DATA

In this stage, the designer of the visualisation acquires, organises, filters, and analyses the data for the visualisations. The misleading tactics happening in this stage refer to all those manipulations on the dataset to convey the intended message.

In a few words: all the problems related to the structure and characteristics of a dataset, like how detailed or precisely the data is grouped or fragmented, errors, spelling mistakes, unclear or confusing categories, characters formatting, and more.

1.1. Flawed information

The data used for the visualisation is not precise in representing the subject observed (e.g. misspellings, lack coherence in collection or measurement, etc).

In the image, 'a' appears to be smaller in quantity than 'b'. However, the data is flawed due to a case-sensitive error. The quantity of 'a' should be combined with 'A', and the corrected total would be higher than 'b'.

1.2. Unclear aggregation

Unclear or non-explicit choices in how the information is aggregated to reduce the size of the dataset (e.g. the visualisation compares amounts, but the timeframe is not explicit).

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The aggregated data shows a trend where men have higher acceptance rates at universities (values in percentage). Yet, this changes when you check the acceptance rates by department values in percentage.
Image from from Correll, M., & Heer, J. (2017) in 'Black Hat Visualization'.

1.3. Omitted/unomitted outliers

Outliers are data points that significantly differ from the majority of the dataset, either by being high or low. Showing extreme outliers can compress the visualisation and obscure the remaining data. Similarly, omitting significant outliers may render certain data trends invisible.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The first picture shows the relationship between two world indicators: energy use and life expectancy. It suggests they are closely connected. However, the second picture, breaking down these two factors for all countries included in the first, reveals that the apparent close link is an illusion caused by including two major outliers: the US and China.
Image from McNutt, A., Kindlmann, G., & Correll, M. (2020). Surfacing Visualization Mirages. Proceedings of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, 1‚Äì16.

1.4. Base rate bias

Comparing absolute values instead of relative ones. This may led to ignore relevant statistical information (the base rate) in favor of case-specific information.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The picture illustrates the difference between comparing absolute values (more vaccinated people in the hospital) and relative ones (unvaccinated people are significantly fewer than vaccinated, yet their hospitalization rate of the total is higher than that for vaccinated people).
Image by MarcRummy.

1.5. Spurious correlations

Refer to a connection between two variables that appears to have a cause-and-effect relationship but has not.

This image correlates two unrelated variables: air quality in Union City and Orlando Bloom's appearances in movies.
Image by Tyler Vigen, you can find more examples here.

1.6. Cherrypicking

Excessive filtering, focused on eliminating irrelevant data or concentrating on individual examples without considering the overall trend.

ü™Ñ 2. VISUALISING DATA

In this stage, the designer decides how to visualise the selected data. The misleading tactics in this stage happen while translating the values in the dataset into a visual representation.

Issues that may emerge when choosing how to visualise the data, such as defining the chart type and all the other elements (e.g. axis parameters, colour schemes, frame dimensions, labels, and legends, etc.).

2.1. Concealed uncertainty

The extent of uncertainty in the visualised data is not shown or explicitly stated. For example, in a hurricane cone visualisation, the cone represents the probable track of the centre of the hurricane. While it may appear precise in indicating the areas potentially being affected, it does not disclose the possible errors or uncertainties in predicting the path.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The image shows a hurricane cone visualisation.
Image from Alberto, 2019. Find more here.

2.2. Chaotic visualisation

The visualisation is excessively complex and may leave the user feeling overwhelmed. This involves charts that are too specific to a particular domain, or visually messy (e.g. labels overlapping, indiscriminable colors, dark grid lines, etc.)

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

In the visualisation, the text labels overlap and are unreadable, making it hard for the reader to extract meaningful information.
Image from Lo, L. Y., Gupta, A., Shigyo, K., Wu, A., Bertini, E., & Qu, H. (2022). Misinformed by Visualization: What Do We Learn From Misinformative Visualizations? Computer Graphics Forum, 41(3), 515‚Äì525. https://doi.org/10.1111/cgf.14559

2.3. Decorative visualisations

Visualisations are used for decorative purposes, either containing no data or only a limited set of information (e.g., a bar chart displaying only one number)

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

This data visualisation is showing just one number, making the bar chart useless for any comparison. This chart is pure decoration.
Image from Correll, M. (2021). Towards a Theory of Bullshit Visualization. ArXiv:2109.12975 [Cs]. http://arxiv.org/abs/2109.12976

2.4. Logarithmic scale

In logarithmic scales, intervals are not equal but increase by a factor of the logarithm's base. This is not an error but has the potential to mislead viewers as it compresses the range of values.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

Being unaware of the logarithmic scale used in this visualization can make readers interpret the curves as flattening.
Image from Lo, L. Y., Gupta, A., Shigyo, K., Wu, A., Bertini, E., & Qu, H. (2022). Misinformed by Visualization: What Do We Learn From Misinformative Visualizations? Computer Graphics Forum, 41(3), 515‚Äì525. https://doi.org/10.1111/cgf.14559

2.5. Inappropriate chart

The selected method for visualising the information is not suitable for the type of data in the dataset (e.g., using a line chart for non-continuous data). For more info and examples on the various methods for data visualisation (or visual model) visit The Dataviz Project.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

A line chart, used for visualising how quantities change along time, is being used to show categorical quantities.
Image from Lo, L. Y., Gupta, A., Shigyo, K., Wu, A., Bertini, E., & Qu, H. (2022). Misinformed by Visualization: What Do We Learn From Misinformative Visualizations? Computer Graphics Forum, 41(3), 515‚Äì525. https://doi.org/10.1111/cgf.14559

2.6. 3D effect

3D charts introduce x, y, and z axes. This can lead to distorted information relationships, hinder value comparison, create challenges in accurate size perception, cause data overlap and visual clutter, and result in non-uniform scaling..

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

For 3D, the closer something is, the larger it appears. In this image the four competitors have actually very similar values, but the 3D perspective exaggerates the perception.
Image from Cairo, A. (2019). How charts lie: Getting smarter about visual information (First edition). W. W. Norton & Company.

2.7. Area encoding

The use of area-encoded marks assumes that readers can visually compare these areas. However, there is a common misunderstanding as these marks are sometimes perceived as encoding length, leading to ambiguity in interpreting the scale. If the chart employs pictograms, it is easier to misunderstand length and areas.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

This chart uses pictograms to encode lenghts, however their area can mislead and exaggerate perception.
Image from Lo, L. Y., Gupta, A., Shigyo, K., Wu, A., Bertini, E., & Qu, H. (2022). Misinformed by Visualization: What Do We Learn From Misinformative Visualizations? Computer Graphics Forum, 41(3), 515‚Äì525. https://doi.org/10.1111/cgf.14559

2.8. Different axis scale

Comparing values with different scales or units. For instance, a dual-axis chart with separate y-axes that share a common x-axis. The two different scales may complicate accurate comparisons.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The chart compares the daily count of COVID-19 cases for countries with mask mandates (orange line) and countries without mask mandates (blue line). The two quantities are on different scales. At first glance, it might seem that the orange line shows a significant decline in COVID-19 cases, lower than the blue line. However, despite appearances, charts with two axes are not designed to show a relationship between two different attributes.
Image from Engledowl, C., & Weiland, T. (2021). Data (Mis)representation and COVID-19: Leveraging Misleading Data Visualizations For Developing Statistical Literacy Across Grades 6‚Äì16. Journal of Statistics and Data Science Education, 29(2), 160‚Äì164. https://doi.org/10.1080/26939169.2021.1915215

2.9. Inverted axis

The configuration of the axes is unconventional and counterintuitive (e.g., smaller value appears at the bottom, progression of time moves right to left, etc.)

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The Y axis in this chart does not start from 0, but from 1000 and decreases from bottom to top.
Image from Reuters.

2.10. Inverted colors

The scale of color saturation and brightness is inverted (e.g., dark or opaque color for smaller numbers and brighter colors for higher magnitudes).

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

In this map, blue is used for small quantities (1 on the scale), while grey is used for larger amounts (50 on the scale). This could lead to misperception, as darker areas might be associated with higher quantities.
Image from Lo, L. Y., Gupta, A., Shigyo, K., Wu, A., Bertini, E., & Qu, H. (2022). Misinformed by Visualization: What Do We Learn From Misinformative Visualizations? Computer Graphics Forum, 41(3), 515‚Äì525. https://doi.org/10.1111/cgf.14559

üíÖüèΩ 3. FINALISING THE DATA VIS

In this stage, the designer improves the output of the previous stage by adding, removing or editing the visual elements. The misleading tactics happening in this stage refer to all those edits made on the visualisation before the final presentation.

Issues in finalising the graphic and style aimed at visual engagement, such as changing colours, adding shapes, legends, texts, annotations, deleting/cutting/inverting axes, etc.

3.1. Visual complexity

The output is extremely intricate, with an excessive number of chaotically arranged elements. This includes unnecessary visual embellishments, superfluous shapes and labels, complex legends, dubious annotations, etc.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì


Image from Kelly, J. (29 April 2020). When data viz goes psychotic. Financial Times.

3.2. Truncated/untruncated axis

The Y or X axes can be truncated to limit the displayed value range or left untruncated to show the full range. Truncating hides a portion of the axis to emphasize or exaggerate differences in the data. Conversely, leaving the axis untruncated may compress data, resulting in a crowded visualization and potential loss of detail.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The two bar charts are visualising the same quantities, but the right bar chart has the Y axis truncated, which leads to message exaggeration.

3.3. Visual alteration

Changes made to an already-finalised visualisation, potentially modifying its meaning and/or introducing new information in a distinct visual style. This can include adding annotations and text, rearranging screenshots of existing charts, altering the colors, introducing arbitrary thresholds, introducing arbitrary labels, rearranging elements

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

The image has annotations and new visual elements that can mislead the understanding of the chart. These edits suggest that the increase in COVID-19 cases in Uruguay was caused by the vaccination campaign.
Image from Lisnic, M., Polychronis, C., Lex, A., & Kogan, M. (2023). Misleading Beyond Visual Tricks: How People Actually Lie with Charts. Proceedings of the 2023 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems, 1‚Äì21. https://doi.org/10.1145/3544548.3580910

3.4. Hidden details

The visualisation omits essential elements for the understandability of the information. For instance: omitting the legend, the data source, the title, subtitle, framing text, the axes labels, or presenting only percentages in a chart and excluding corresponding numerical values, etc.

Expand to see an example ‚Üì

Without the title, the legend, and the data source, this data visualisation is unreadable.
Image from VisLies, 2023.
